Lab 0: Introduction to ADQL and the Gaia Data Release 2¶

1. Introduction¶

We will in this report study three known clusters, using the newly released Gaia Data Release 2. After locating and identifying all assumed member of the clusters, we will construct a color - absolute magnitude (HR) digram for each cluster, hopefully resulting in a nice isochrone.

To identify the members of each cluster, we will use known values for their angular location, parallax, and proper motion. After studying the behaviour of these parameters around the known values, we will apply resonable filters to their values, in order to pick out the cluster bodies.

In addition, we will employ some relatively strict noise-reducing filters, to remove any potentially bad data. As our final goal is not to constuct a most complete set of likely cluster members, but to construct an accurate isochrone, quality beats quantity.

1.1 The clusters and their properties¶

The clusters we will study are

  1. The Hyades: An open, young cluster, only an estimated 625 million years old. It is also the clostest of the three, at only 47 pc.
  2. M67 (or NGC 2682): An open, much older cluster, estimated to be somewhere from 3 to 5 billion years old. It is also much farther away, at over 800 pc.
  3. NGC 6397: A globular cluster, even older, somewhere around 13 billion years old. Also the most remote, at 2.4kpc.

We start this lab by gathering some data about each cluster, which will all be usefull in our analysis. Most data is gathered from reputable papers on the Gaia DR2.

2. Data collection and initial filtering¶

2.1 Noise Filters¶

The Gaia Data Release 2 contains well over a billion celestial bodies. The accuracy of measurement will necessarily vary widely from object to object. As our aim is to derive an isocrone as accurate as possible, quality beats quantity, and we will employ a series of filters aimed at removing potentially bad data. The filters are found in the appendix of Babusiaux et al.[2], and are described in section 2.1 of the paper.

The effect of some of the most important filters are listed here.

  • visibility_periods_used: To my understanding, this is the number of "independent" observations of the object for at least 4 days, separated from other objects.
  • parallax_over_error: The ratio of parallax to parallax_error is required to be above a factor 10.
  • phot_**_mean_flux_over_error: Similar requirements to the errors of other parameters.
  • phot_bp_rp_excess_factor and phot_bp_mean_mag-phot_rp_mean_mag: This is an attempt to reduce noise and blur from nearby and background light-sources.

The filters seem well fit for relatively close and open clusters, where noise levels are reasonably low, and parallax measurements are accurate. We will loosen the filter values somewhat in the globular, farther away cluster.

In a random circular section, the number of extracted celestial bodies fell from 13'000 to 3500 upon applying the suggested filters from the paper, removing almost four of every five stars. They will, however, leave us with a much higher quality data-set. The ratio of removed bodies seems to be proportional to distance from earth, as expected.

We apply these filters into an ADQL query, and access the Gaia servers with the astroquer package, as shown below. We will later append cluster-specific filters to this query.

2.2 Cluster ra/dec filtering and queries¶

We will now pull down Gaia data in the approximate "angular area" of the three clusters (right ascension and declination). The angles are chosen very liberally, around twice the assumed size of the clusters, to ensure we get the entire clusters. We will instead employ stricter parallax and proper motion cuts, to ensure only cluster members are chosen.

While most information we desire is directly avaliable as columns in the Gaia archive, we will constuct the Gaia magnitude mg as phot_g_mean_mag+5*log10(parallax)-10, as suggested in Babusiaux et al. 2018[2].

2.2.1 Hyades Query¶

The Hyades has a known apparent dimension of around 5 degrees, and we choose to employ a comfortable 10 degree circle (5 degree radius) around its estimated center.

In [2]:
Hyades_ADQL_query="""
SELECT ra, ra_error, dec, dec_error, pmra, pmdec, parallax, parallax_error, phot_g_mean_mag+5*log10(parallax)-10 AS mg, bp_rp
FROM gaiadr2.gaia_source
WHERE
    parallax_over_error > 10
AND phot_g_mean_flux_over_error>50
AND phot_rp_mean_flux_over_error>20
AND phot_bp_mean_flux_over_error>20
AND phot_bp_rp_excess_factor < 1.3+0.06*power(phot_bp_mean_mag-phot_rp_mean_mag,2)
AND phot_bp_rp_excess_factor > 1.0+0.015*power(phot_bp_mean_mag-phot_rp_mean_mag,2)
AND visibility_periods_used>8
AND astrometric_chi2_al/(astrometric_n_good_obs_al-5)<1.44*greatest(1,exp(-0.4*(phot_g_mean_mag-19.5)))
AND CONTAINS(
        POINT('ICRS', gaiadr2.gaia_source.ra, gaiadr2.gaia_source.dec),
        CIRCLE('ICRS', 66.725, 15.86667, 5)
    )=1
"""

job = gaia.launch_job_async(Hyades_ADQL_query)
data_Hyades = job.get_results()

Query finished.

2.2.3 NGC 6397 Query¶

The NGC 6397 also has an apparent dimension of around 1/2 degree, and we again employ a 1 degree search around its estimated center.

We relax the parallax_over_error condition to a ratio of 4, as suggested in A. Helmi et al. 2018[3], section 2.1 on filtering of globular clusters(the paper reduced it all the way down to 2, which we believe is a bit too generious, as it gave much more noise to the final solution). We have also slightly reduced the ratios for other error tolerances.

In [4]:
NGC6397_ADQL_query="""
SELECT ra, ra_error, dec, dec_error, pmra, pmdec, parallax, parallax_error, phot_g_mean_mag+5*log10(parallax)-10 AS mg, bp_rp
FROM gaiadr2.gaia_source
WHERE
    parallax_over_error > 4
AND phot_g_mean_flux_over_error>35
AND phot_rp_mean_flux_over_error>15
AND phot_bp_mean_flux_over_error>15

AND CONTAINS(
        POINT('ICRS', gaiadr2.gaia_source.ra, gaiadr2.gaia_source.dec),

4. HR Diagrams and Conclusion¶

We now turn to the main target of this lab, the construction of color - magnitude diagrams for the three clusters. For our Gaia data, this is very straightforward, as we have direct acces to magnitude and R-B data.

For comparison, we will use theoretical isochrones from the MIST isochrone generator, using log(age) and Fe/H values provided in table 1. We have also input a correction for extinction (the absorption and scattering of light off interstellar dust). For Hyades and M67, we used $E(B-V)$ values from our table 1, and converted them to $A_v$ using the relation $A_v = 3.2E(B-V)$, resulting in values of $0.0032$ and $0.184$, respectively. For NGC6397, the value $A_v = 0.56$ was retrived from Hong Tsui, Decomposition of the Globular Cluster NGC 6397. We set the output to Gaia Synthetic Photometry. The output columns contain values for both R-B color data, as well as absolute magnitude.

In [28]:
def get_isochrone(filename):
    """Returns an isochrone (bp_rp & mg) from a MIST generated .cmd isochrone file."""
    bp_rp = []
    mg = []
    with open(filename, "r") as infile:
        for i in range(13):  # Skipping info lines.
            infile.readline()
        for line in infile:
            words = line.split()
            mg.append(float(words[22]))
            bp_rp.append(float(words[23])-float(words[24]))
    bp_rp = np.array(bp_rp)
    mg = np.array(mg)
    return bp_rp, mg

4.1 Hyades¶

From the figure below, we see that the Hyades cluster creates a thin, welldefined strip along the main-sequence isochrone. Both its length and lack of width confirms its young age. The data falls slightly above the theoretical isochrone. The isochrone of a slightly older cluster would be skewed this way, but it would also crumble it's top-left tail further down, where we see that the data fits quite well. A right-shift of the isochrone is also accomplished by a higher assumed extinction level, but at the cluster is very close to earth, this seems unlikely.

Another possible reason is a higher than assumed metallicity, $Fe/H$, than assumed. As seen in the figure below, in increase in the metallicity from $0.13$ to $0.25$ gives a better fit.

Neither of these explanations are very satisfactory however, as all these parameters are already estiblished by many astronomers. The real explanation is likely something more mundane, like the lack of account for other variables, or simply inaccuracy in our isochrone generator(or data).

The cluster does either way form a nice thin line due to its age, making us able to clearly observe a handful of binary stars, which lie scattered slightly above the data's isochrone. There appears to be at least 5 or 10 in our current dataset, although it should be mentioned that binary stars are more often lost than their single simblings during data cleansing, as outlines in section 2.1 of Babusiaux et al. 2018[2].

4.2 M67¶

Below we see the HR diagram of the M67 cluster. In similar fashion to the Hyades cluster, the data falls slightly above (albeit touching) the theoretical isochrone line. This could also be explained by one of the factors mentioned above. Some bodies also lie scattered left and above the isochrone, and probably does not belong to the cluster, but a main-series isochrone. The upper tail("turn-off point") of the data fits very well with the isochrone, bending in the exact same way. We see the cluster is evolving away from the main sequence, and stars have started evolving into giants.

Some binary stars seem very plausible, although due to the higher level of spread and noise, it is harder to say for sure.

Finally, comparing to the HR diagram without a proper motion cut, there is not really that much difference, perhaps suggesting we could have been even more strict in our cuts.

4.3 NGC6397¶

The NGC6397 cluster shows a rather different shape than the two open clusters, many stars having moved away from the main sequence. We see that the upper left portion of the main sequence is entirely gone, having evolved into giants, and some bodies having moved all the way over to the horizontal branch.

The original isochrone fit for the NGC6397 was particularly peculiar, cutting off very low at the main sequence, and stopping there. Increasing the Fe/H ratio slightly created a much better fit, showing the raminding main sequence, the giants, and the horizontal branch.

Final Notes¶

Taking a final look at the total number of celestial bodies observed in each cluster, we can see that our own estimate varies from 12% to 61% of established number of bodies. That our hit-rate is low in the further, globular cluster, is no surprise. That the M67 has such a good hit-rate (compared to Hyades) is however quite interesting. We might have picket up some non-cluster celestial bodies, as the HR diagram is somewhat noisy, and we would not really expect it to have a higher hit-rate than Hyades, with its very clean parallax cut. We did, however, keep the noise filters the same as Hyades, even though M67 is substansially further away.

For the NGC 6397, there is no doubt that the noise filters picked away a lot of bodies, as we are left with only half its supposed number of stars after only the angular position cut.

One other expected remark is the extreme effectiveness of the Parallax cut on Hyades, reducing the number of bodies by 99.7%.

Future Work¶

We could definitively have run our sanity checks also in proper motion and angular position space, to confirm uniformity.

The cuts in parallax, angle and proper motion, while effective enough, were nothing more that qualified guesses from a human eye. More powerful methods of cluster-searching should definitively be employed in the future.
